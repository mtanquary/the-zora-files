Foreword

Foreword

The Chronicle Begins

"Every dawn is a scored event. Most people sleep through the leaderboard."

This is not a travel guide. It is not a photography tutorial. It is not a nature journal. It is a game, played at altitudes where the air is thin and the light is honest, in the dark hours before the world decides what kind of day it wants to be.

The game has a name: Finding Zora. Zora is the Slavic word for dawn: that specific shade of possibility that exists only in the window between absolute darkness and the moment the sun commits to rising. Every expedition in this chronicle is an attempt to find it in its purest form.

The game has rules. It has a scoring system. It has a leaderboard, a discovery log, and a leveling system that rewards the player for pushing further, arriving earlier, and seeing more. It has a theoretical maximum score that may never be reached. That's the point.

This rulebook is the authoritative reference for how scores are calculated, how discoveries are logged, how records are set, and how a player advances through the ranks. It is designed to be adopted by any individual player, group, or community who wants a structured, repeatable framework for the pursuit. One player or many. Any landscape. Any latitude. The rules apply wherever dawn breaks.

The game has no fixed home. It has been played in desert canyons and coastal cliffs, on mountain ridgelines and river banks, in landscapes the scorpions know and in latitudes they have never visited. Wherever the sun rises, the scoring system applies. The leaderboard is yours to build.

One final note before the rules begin. The scoring system rewards effort and intentionality, not luck. A spectacular sky witnessed from a hotel parking lot will always score lower than a modest sky earned at the top of a difficult pre-dawn ascent. The game knows the difference. So does the player.

Let the record show that every pursuit begins somewhere, before anyone is watching, in the dark.

Chapter I

The Two Pillars

"The Eos Index measures what the sky offers. The Zora Score measures what you deserved to see it."

Every expedition in Finding Zora produces two numbers. These numbers are distinct, serve different purposes, and together tell the complete story of a chase.

100
Eos Index · max score
The pure quality score of the sunrise itself. Sky, setting, and conditions combine to produce a number between 0 and 100. This is what the dawn gave you.
~215
Zora Score · theoretical max
The full expedition score. The Eos Index is the foundation, and travel, discovery, elevation, and adversity stack on top. This is what you earned.
The Master Formula
Zora Score = Eos Index + Discovery Pts + Travel Pts + Conditions Pts Theoretical maximum: approximately 215 points · A perfect 100 Eos Index is the ultimate sub-objective

Both scores are recorded at the conclusion of every expedition. Both appear on the leaderboard. Players who care about photography tend to follow the Eos Index. Players who care about the full adventure track the Zora Score. The game accommodates both orientations simultaneously.

Core Principle

A perfect sunrise witnessed from a parking lot will always score lower than a modest sunrise earned at the top of a difficult pre-dawn ascent. The Eos Index measures the sky. The Zora Score measures the player.

Chapter II

The Eos Index

"Eos, goddess of dawn, does not grade on a curve. Neither does this index."

The Eos Index is a scored assessment of sunrise quality, calculated across three components: Sky, Setting, and Conditions. The maximum possible score is 100 points. A score of 100 represents the theoretical perfect sunrise, the destination the game is named for.

Scores are assessed by the player in the field and may be finalized on review of any documentation gathered during the expedition: photographs, notes, and recorded observations. No score may be retroactively improved, but may be retroactively lowered if a review reveals an error in the original field assessment.

50
Sky
Color, cloud engagement, and horizon clarity. The dominant component.
30
Setting
Foreground composition and uniqueness of location.
20
Conditions
Effort to reach the position and environmental challenge.
Sky Component

I. Sky · 50 Points Maximum

The sky is the game. Everything else is context for the sky.

Sky Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts What earns a high score What earns a low score
Color Intensity 20 Deep reds, vivid oranges, saturated pinks across a wide chromatic range of the visible sky Pale yellow wash, minimal hue variation, muted or washed-out tones
Cloud Engagement 15 Dramatically lit clouds with visible rim-lighting, crepuscular rays, or layered cloud structures catching the first light Completely clear sky (moderate penalty) or flat uniform overcast with no visible detail (severe penalty)
Horizon Definition 15 Clean, readable horizon with clear separation between sky strata and earth: mountains, ridgelines, or open water at the edge Obstructed horizon (urban skyline, heavy haze), or no discernible horizon at all

Note on clear skies: a cloudless sunrise is not automatically a perfect sky. Clouds are the canvas upon which dawn paints her most memorable work. A perfectly clear sky earns a ceiling of approximately 35 out of 50 in the Sky component. The remaining 15 require clouds.

Setting Component

II. Setting · 30 Points Maximum

Where you are when the sun rises matters as much as what the sky does. An extraordinary sunrise witnessed from an extraordinary location is the mark of a well-planned expedition.

Setting Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts High score Low score
Foreground Composition 15 Compelling natural subject in frame: rock formation, ancient tree, waterfall, wildlife, or water reflection creating visual depth between viewer and horizon Featureless foreground, asphalt, suburban elements such as fences or buildings
Location Uniqueness 15 Remote wilderness, geological rarity, historically significant site, or a location requiring deliberate effort to access Suburban street, roadside pull-off, hotel parking lot, or any location accessible to the general public without planning
Conditions Component

III. Conditions · 20 Points Maximum

The Conditions component of the Eos Index specifically measures the effort to reach the chosen sunrise position and the environmental challenge encountered in doing so. This is distinct from the travel scoring in the Zora Score. This measures only the final approach and the conditions at the location itself.

Conditions Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts High score Low score
Effort to Reach Position 10 Serious pre-dawn hike with elevation gain, technical terrain, or navigation challenge to reach the sunrise position Drive to location and stand beside the vehicle; roadside pull-off; flat paved walk under 0.25 miles
Environmental Challenge 10 Extreme cold, high wind, technical footing, rain, snow, or conditions that required genuine preparation and risk assessment Comfortable weather, no wind, clear footing: a perfect morning in every way except the challenge

Chapter III

The Zora Score

"The sky rewards those who showed up. The Zora Score rewards those who had no business being there at all."

The Zora Score is the complete expedition score. It begins with the Eos Index and accumulates additional points across five categories: Travel, Elevation, Pre-Dawn Timing, Weather Adversity, and Discovery. Discovery points are covered in full in Chapter IV.

Travel Points

I. Travel Distance

Measured from the player's home base or overnight location to the sunrise position, by the most direct navigable route.

Pre-Dawn Timing

IV. Pre-Dawn Arrival Bonus

Measured as the number of minutes between arrival at the sunrise position and official sunrise time. Verified by camera metadata timestamp compared against the official sunrise time for that GPS coordinate on that date.

Pre-Dawn Arrival Bonus

Verification Protocol

The first photo or video timestamp recorded at the sunrise position, compared against the official sunrise time for those coordinates and that date (sourced from timeanddate.com), determines the arrival bonus. Camera clocks must be synced to accurate network time before each expedition. Disputed timestamps default to 0 bonus points.

Weather Adversity

V. Weather Adversity

Self-assessed at the time of the expedition and supported by any documentation gathered. A single point on this scale is selected per expedition; these do not stack.

Weather Adversity Scale
ConditionsPoints
Perfect conditions: calm, comfortable, unremarkable morning0 pts
Notable conditions: significant cold, sustained wind, or unseasonable weather, but manageable+1 pt
Challenging conditions: precipitation (rain or snow), restricted visibility, or conditions requiring protective gear+2 pts
Severe conditions: expedition was genuinely at risk of abandonment, with obvious environmental challenge on record+3 pts
Streak Bonus

VI. Consecutive Expedition Streak

One bonus point is awarded for every four consecutive expeditions completed on a consistent cadence. The streak resets to zero if an outing is missed or significantly delayed. This is the smallest bonus in the system and the hardest to maintain.

Streak Definition

"On a consistent cadence" means expeditions completed no more than 21 days apart. Players who publish a public record may define their own cadence at the start of a season; the streak requires adherence to that declared schedule. The streak counter should be visible in whatever tracking system the player maintains.

Chapter IV

The Discovery Log

"You came for the sunrise. You stayed for the coyote. The ledger records both."

The Discovery Log is the living record of every species, geological formation, cultural site, and natural feature encountered across the pursuit. Every first encounter is an Unlock. Every Unlock adds points to the Zora Score of the expedition in which it occurs. Every Unlock is permanent and cannot be erased from the record.

The Discovery Log functions as a growing world map of encounters. Over the life of the pursuit, it becomes a documentary record of the natural and human history witnessed during the chase. This is where the game becomes something more than a game.

Discovery Categories

I. Four Discovery Categories

Discovery Category Reference
CategoryExamples
Wildlife Birds, mammals, reptiles, amphibians, insects: any living fauna encountered and documented
Flora Notable, rare, or species-specific plants: a desert wildflower bloom, an old-growth specimen, an endemic plant outside its known range
Geographic Geological formations, water bodies, natural arches, caves, lava fields, slot canyons: features of the earth itself
Cultural & Historical Petroglyphs, ruins, abandoned structures, ceremonial sites, mining history, indigenous land markers: evidence of human presence through time
Rarity Tiers

II. Rarity Tiers and Point Values

Every discovery is assigned a rarity tier at the time of logging. Tier assignment is based on regional rarity, difficulty of encounter, and documentary significance. Point values are fixed at the time of first unlock and cannot be changed retroactively.

Rarity Tier System
TierFirst UnlockSubsequent FindsWildlife Examples
Common 5 – 10 pts No repeat scoring Common Sparrow, Cottontail Rabbit, Mourning Dove
Uncommon 15 – 20 pts No repeat scoring Red-tailed Hawk, Great Blue Heron, White-tailed Deer, River Otter
Rare 25 – 35 pts +1 pt per outing Osprey, Peregrine Falcon, Bobcat, River Turtle
Very Rare 40 – 50 pts +1 pt per outing Bald Eagle, Gray Wolf, American Black Bear, Mountain Lion
Exceptional 60 – 75 pts +1 pt per outing Once-per-pursuit encounters, rare enough that no list can anticipate them
The Repeat Rule

Common and Uncommon discoveries score only on first encounter. Rare, Very Rare, and Exceptional discoveries earn one additional point for each subsequent encounter, provided the encounter occurs on a separate, distinct outing. Multiple encounters during the same expedition count as a single encounter.

III. Cultural and Historical Sites

Cultural and historical discoveries are scored on significance rather than rarity tier. The following scale applies to first encounter only. Subsequent visits to the same site do not score additional points.

IV. How an Unlock Is Logged

The following is an example of a properly logged discovery entry as it would appear in the Discovery Log:

Documentation Standard

A discovery is only eligible for points if it is documented with photographic or video evidence sufficient for confident identification. Blurry or inconclusive footage may be supported by audio recording (bird calls, etc.) in combination. The standard is: could a qualified naturalist confirm this identification from the footage provided?

Chapter V

The Records Board

"Records exist to be broken. That is the only reason the board is kept."

The Records Board tracks the all-time bests across a defined set of categories. Unlike the Eos Index leaderboard (which ranks all expeditions by sunrise quality) and the Zora Score leaderboard (which ranks all expeditions by total score), Records track specific achievements that exist outside the scoring system.

A record, once set, belongs to the expedition that set it. When a record is broken, the previous holder is noted. Records tell the story of the pursuit's ambition over time; they are the chronicle of how far the pursuit has ranged.

Chapter VI

The Leveling System

"Every six outings, if the player has shown up and done the work, something changes. A new tool arrives. A new range becomes possible. The game expands."

The Leveling System is the long arc of the game. Where the Eos Index measures a single dawn and the Zora Score measures a single expedition, the Level measures the cumulative weight of all expeditions: every predawn alarm, every cold trail, every sunrise logged in the record.

Every level earned is a milestone. What that milestone means in practice is entirely up to the player. It might mean investing in new equipment, planning a more ambitious expedition, or simply acknowledging that the pursuit has deepened. The leveling system provides structure and cadence; the player decides what crossing each threshold is worth to them. Better tools and greater range expand what the Eos Index can reach. The higher the Eos Index, the faster the leveling. The loop rewards commitment.

Level-Up Ceremony

III. The Level-Up Moment

When a level threshold is crossed, the player acknowledges it in the outing or record where the cumulative Zora Score first clears the threshold. Formally, the level-up moment includes the following:

  1. Current cumulative Zora Score noted
  2. Threshold crossed, recorded in the log
  3. New level title claimed
  4. Personal milestone acknowledged: the player decides what this level means to them and what, if anything, to invest or do differently as a result
  5. Brief reflection on what the next level requires and what it will enable

Players who document their pursuit publicly may choose to announce level-ups as part of their record. The level and progress toward the next threshold should be visible in whatever tracking system the player maintains.

Chapter VII

Field Protocol

"The protocol exists because the wilderness does not wait for the unready. Set your clock the night before."

The Field Protocol governs the structure of every expedition. It is not a rigid script (the wilderness does not honor scripts), but a framework that ensures scoring integrity, consistent documentation quality, and the best possible chance of witnessing something worth recording.

Pre-Expedition

I. The Night Before

  1. Confirm sunrise time for the target GPS coordinates and date
  2. Check weather forecast: note cloud cover probability (moderate coverage optimizes Eos Index; clear is moderate; full overcast is poor)
  3. Check moon phase: a dark moon window produces better pre-dawn ambient conditions
  4. Research recent wildlife sightings for the target location if resources are available. Know what's possible before arrival
  5. Charge all equipment the night before: cameras, lights, and any recording devices
  6. Lay out gear and clothing. Nothing gets searched for in the dark at 4AM
  7. Set two alarms: one at wake time, one five minutes later
Departure

II. Departing for the Location

  1. Sync all device clocks to accurate network time before leaving. This is the single most important technical step of the expedition; timestamp integrity determines pre-dawn arrival bonus verification.
  2. Begin passive sound or wildlife logging as early as possible. Many players use identification apps that log audio or observations automatically.
  3. Record or note starting conditions: current time, target sunrise time, weather, and your location origin. This creates a timestamped record that supports scoring in the field assessment.
The Discovery Window

III. Pre-Dawn Discovery Window

The period between arrival and sunrise is the primary discovery window. Wildlife and features encountered here score as the expedition's discovery points.

  1. Move slowly and deliberately; high-movement approaches clear wildlife from the area
  2. Photograph or document every potential discovery before attempting identification
  3. Log all wildlife and plant sightings in real time. Do not rely on memory
  4. Note GPS coordinates for significant geographic features
  5. Reach the designated sunrise position with a minimum of 30 minutes remaining before sunrise
The Sunrise

IV. Sunrise Protocol

  1. Lock down your primary camera on a stable mount, facing east, minimum 20 minutes before sunrise
  2. Lock your exposure settings: fix white balance, frame rate, and exposure on the sky near the horizon so the recording does not drift as the sky brightens
  3. Begin your continuous sunrise recording. Do not interrupt this for any reason short of equipment failure. This is the Eos Index source material.
  4. Use any secondary cameras for supplementary coverage while the primary runs
  5. Note your real-time field assessment of sky conditions; this becomes your preliminary Eos Index score and a reference for the formal assessment
The Verdict

V. The Verdict

The formal closing assessment of every expedition. Contains the following in order:

  1. Eos Index breakdown: Sky, Setting, Conditions scores stated individually with brief justification
  2. Eos Index total: where it sits on the all-time leaderboard
  3. Discovery summary: all unlocks from this expedition, point values, running total
  4. Travel and conditions points: brief summary
  5. Zora Score total: this expedition's final score
  6. Cumulative Zora Score: running total, progress toward next level
  7. Level-up acknowledgement: if applicable, see Chapter VI
  8. Records broken: if applicable
On Disputed Scores

The player is the sole authority on all scores. The scoring system is documented and transparent, but the player's field assessment is final. Arithmetic errors may be corrected at any time, with a note made in the record. No score may be retroactively improved; only corrected or lowered.

Appendix B

Glossary of Terms

"Know the language of the pursuit."

Blue Hour
The 20–30 minute window before sunrise when the sky transitions from deep navy to pale gold. Often more visually striking than sunrise itself. The primary pre-positioning window for the player to reach and settle into their sunrise location.
Calibration Session
An optional pre-season scoring exercise in which existing photographs or documentation are scored using the Eos Index rubric to establish personal leaderboard benchmarks and test scoring consistency before formal play begins.
Cumulative Zora Score
The running total of all Zora Scores across all expeditions. Determines the player's current Level. Should be tracked in whatever record system the player maintains.
Discovery Window
The period between arrival at a location and official sunrise time. The primary window for wildlife encounters and feature discovery. Extended pre-dawn arrivals produce longer discovery windows.
Eos Index
The pure sunrise quality score for a single episode. Scored across three components (Sky, Setting, Conditions) with a maximum of 100 points. Named for Eos, the Greek goddess of dawn.
Episode Score
Synonymous with Zora Score for a specific expedition. Each outing produces both an Eos Index and a Zora Score.
Exceptional Tier
The highest rarity tier in the Discovery Log. Encounters so rare that no definitive list can anticipate them. Worth 60–75 points on first unlock. Examples may only become clear in the moment of encounter.
Finding Zora
The name of this game system and the pursuit it governs. Also the name of Level 10, the theoretical culmination of the pursuit. Also the ultimate objective of the Eos Index: a perfect score of 100. A perfect sunrise.
First Unlock
The first confirmed encounter with a species, feature, or cultural site that has not previously appeared in the Discovery Log. Earns the full point value for that rarity tier. Each species, site, or feature can only produce one First Unlock.
Home Base
The player's primary residence or established origin point. Used as the geographic starting point for travel distance calculations when no overnight location closer to the expedition has been established.
The Level-Up Moment
The formal acknowledgement when a Level threshold is crossed. Includes recording the new level title, noting the cumulative score that cleared the threshold, and reflecting on the personal milestone the player has chosen to associate with this level.
The Records Board
The all-time best achievements tracking board, separate from the Eos Index and Zora Score leaderboards. Tracks non-scoring achievements such as highest elevation, longest streak, and most unusual location.
The Verdict
The structured closing assessment of every expedition where all scores are announced, discoveries logged, and progress updated. The formal record of what the expedition produced.
Unlock
A first confirmed encounter with a species, geographic feature, or cultural site. Once unlocked, an entry appears permanently in the Discovery Log.
Zora
The Slavic word for dawn. The name of the pursuit, the game, and the theoretical destination at the end of the leaderboard. A perfect sunrise. The one that scores 100.
Zora Score
The complete expedition score for a single outing. The Eos Index is the foundation; travel points, elevation points, discovery points, pre-dawn arrival bonus, weather adversity, and streak bonus stack on top. Theoretical maximum: approximately 215 points.

Appendix C

House Rules

"The rules exist. They also bend. Know the difference."

The standard rulebook is designed to work as written, for any player, in any landscape, without modification. But the game also acknowledges that players arrive with different histories, play in different configurations, and may want to adjust the structure to fit their pursuit. The following house rules are officially recognized variants. Any house rule in use should be declared at the start of a season and noted in the player's record.

Starting Level
House Rule · Starting Level Declaration

A player with documented prior experience in wilderness navigation, wildlife identification, or dawn photography may declare a Starting Level at the beginning of their first season rather than starting at Scout. Starting Level may be declared at Level 1 or 2 by solo declaration. Levels 3 and above require a formal Calibration Session (see below) to establish the opening cumulative Zora Score. Starting Level reflects experience, not points already banked; the player still begins their cumulative score at zero unless a Calibration Session produces a nonzero total.

Calibration as Season Zero
House Rule · Calibration as Season Zero

A player with an existing library of documented sunrise expeditions may run a Season Zero before formal play begins. In Season Zero, the player scores up to ten prior expeditions under the full rulebook to establish an opening cumulative Zora Score. All standard rules apply. The resulting score determines the player's starting level and acts as the baseline leaderboard for Season One. Season Zero expeditions are marked as calibration in the log and do not count toward the streak.

Group Play
House Rule · Group Play

When two or more players undertake the same expedition, each player maintains their own Eos Index assessment and Zora Score. Travel, elevation, and pre-dawn arrival are identical for all players on a shared expedition. Weather adversity is assessed individually. The Discovery Log may be maintained as either individual (each player logs their own first-unlock entries independently) or shared (the group maintains a single communal log, and first-unlock points are awarded to the player who first documented the encounter). Declare the variant before the expedition begins. Switching mid-season is not permitted.

Season Definition
House Rule · Season Definition

The standard game is continuous, with no defined season boundaries. Players who prefer a seasonal structure may declare a season as any contiguous period of active play, typically three to twelve months. At the start of each new season, the player may reset the streak counter, define a new streak cadence, and optionally add new Records Board categories. The cumulative Zora Score and Discovery Log carry forward between seasons and are never reset. Seasons are cosmetic structure; they do not affect scoring.

Photography-Only Mode
House Rule · Photography-Only Mode

Players focused exclusively on sunrise quality rather than the full expedition challenge may elect to play in Photography-Only Mode. In this variant, only the Eos Index is scored. Travel points, elevation points, discovery points, pre-dawn bonus, weather adversity, and streak bonus are not tracked. The leaderboard consists solely of Eos Index scores. This mode is well suited to players who are stationary observers, photographers working a single location over time, or groups that want a common scoring language without the logistics of full expedition play.

Discovery Disputes
House Rule · Discovery Tier Disputes

When a player is uncertain of the appropriate rarity tier for a discovery, the default is to assign conservatively (the lower of the two candidate tiers) and note the dispute in the log. The assignment may be revised upward at any time with additional documentation or corroborating expert confirmation. Revisions downward are also permitted at any time. In group play, tier disputes are resolved by majority vote among active players; ties default to the lower tier. A tier assignment that has been formally recorded and announced in a public log may not be revised upward after more than thirty days from the original entry.

Appendix D

The Expedition Planner

"The sunrise happens whether you planned for it or not. The score reflects which one you were."

The following tips are not rules. Nothing here affects scoring. They are the accumulated wisdom of the pursuit, organized by when they are most useful. A well-planned expedition is not guaranteed to produce a great sunrise. But it consistently produces a better one than the alternative.

The Week Before

Location Scouting

The single highest-leverage planning activity is choosing the right position before you leave. Tools worth using:

  • Google Earth / satellite view to assess terrain, tree cover, and horizon obstructions before committing to a location. What looks open on a map is often blocked by a ridge or a tree line in reality.
  • PhotoPills or a sun position app to establish exactly where on the horizon the sun will rise at your target location on your target date. The sun's azimuth shifts significantly across the year; a position that framed the sun perfectly in October may completely miss it in March.
  • AllTrails, Gaia GPS, or recent trip reports for access route conditions, especially for off-pavement approaches. A locked gate or a washed-out road wastes the whole alarm.
  • County road department sites for current road condition reports if you're planning high-clearance terrain. Call if in doubt; the record does not reward a recovery story that started before dawn.

Wildlife Intelligence

  • eBird for recent bird sightings at or near your target location. Filter by date range to understand what species are currently active in the area, not just what has been recorded historically.
  • iNaturalist for broader fauna and flora observations. Check the last 30 days of sightings within a few miles of your target to build a picture of what you might realistically encounter.
  • If you are targeting a specific rare species, research its known behavioral patterns. Many highly-scorable encounters happen to players who knew where to look and moved into position early.
The Night Before

Reading the Sky Forecast

Cloud cover is not the enemy. It is the variable. Understanding it is the difference between showing up and showing up at the right time.

  • 30 to 60 percent cloud coverage is the Eos Index sweet spot. Enough cloud structure to catch and scatter the light; not so much that the horizon disappears into grey.
  • Clear skies produce clean, predictable sunrises with a ceiling of roughly 35 out of 50 in the Sky component. Beautiful but not dramatic.
  • Full overcast is the worst-case sky scenario. Pursue anyway if the setting and conditions points are strong. A 20-point sky with a 28-point setting and 18-point conditions is still a 66. That is a respectable expedition.
  • Windfinder, Ventusky, or Weather Underground offer more granular localized forecasts than standard weather apps, especially for elevated or coastal positions.

Moon Phase

A new moon window produces a darker pre-dawn sky, cleaner ambient light, and generally higher wildlife activity. A full moon produces a lit landscape that can add genuine visual interest to pre-dawn photography, especially on snow or water. Neither is better; both are worth understanding before you go.

The Gear Audit

Nothing on the checklist is optional if it is on the checklist. Batteries that were not charged the night before have a pattern of being dead at 5AM. The gear audit is not about perfection; it is about eliminating the preventable failures that cost points and create frustration.

Departure Day

Leave-Time Math

Work backwards from your target position time. Build in a minimum 30-minute buffer beyond your best estimate of travel time. Off-pavement and high-clearance routes take longer in the dark. The pre-dawn arrival bonus requires 30 to 60 minutes on position before sunrise. A player who budgets exactly enough time earns zero bonus points; a player who budgets generously earns two. The math favors the early alarm.

The Commit Point

On long approaches, identify in advance the distance or time at which turning back is no longer worth the cost. Once past the commit point, the expedition continues regardless of conditions changing. Knowing this in advance prevents the hesitation that costs both time and resolve on the road.

Passive Logging from the Car

If you use a passive wildlife identification app (Merlin Bird ID is the most useful for audio), start it before you leave the vehicle. The drive-in window often produces audible species that would be missed once you are focused on positioning. Any documented encounter from the moment you depart your origin point is eligible for the Discovery Log.

In the Field

Stillness is the Skill

More wildlife encounters are created by staying still than by covering ground. A player who reaches their position early and remains still and quiet for 45 minutes will typically see more than a player who spent those 45 minutes moving through the same area. The pre-dawn arrival bonus rewards this behavior directly; the Discovery Log rewards it indirectly.

The 360 Scan

Do not only look east. The sunrise is behind you for part of the morning. The light it casts on the landscape to the west, north, and south is often the most photographically interesting element of the expedition. Wildlife does not respect your horizon orientation. Significant discoveries happen at the edges of where the player was looking.

Sound Before Sight

In low light, most wildlife encounters are heard before they are seen. Train yourself to pause and listen before scanning visually. A call, a rustle, a splash, or a wing beat often precedes a documentable encounter by 10 to 30 seconds. Players who move too quickly through the landscape consistently miss the audio window.

Know Your Fallback Position

Before committing fully to a primary sunrise position, identify a secondary position within reasonable reach. If conditions at the primary are worse than anticipated (horizon blocked by cloud bank, position already occupied, unexpected obstruction), a known fallback prevents the decision paralysis that wastes the most valuable minutes of the expedition.

Post-Expedition

Log Within 24 Hours

Memory of specific field details degrades faster than most players expect. The quality of the Discovery Log over the life of the pursuit depends almost entirely on the discipline of logging soon after each expedition. A discovery entry written three days later is missing details that felt unforgettable in the field.

The Photo Review

Images captured in the field often contain detail the eye missed in the moment. Review all captures before finalizing your Eos Index score. A cloud formation that seemed moderate may reveal dramatic structure on screen. A background element that did not register in the field may resolve into a documentable species. The Eos Index may be assessed on review of any documentation gathered during the expedition; use this permission deliberately.

Run Identification Apps on Unknowns

Do not let unidentified captures expire. Any species photographed during an expedition that was not positively identified in the field should be submitted to iNaturalist or a comparable identification service before the next expedition. A confirmed identification can claim the discovery point value retroactively provided it occurred within the same expedition window.

Appendix E

Your First Expedition

"You do not need perfect conditions. You need to be somewhere with a clear view east before the sun rises. Everything else is optional the first time."

You have read the rulebook. The scoring system may feel large. It is not complicated in practice. Your first expedition does not need to produce a great score. It needs to produce a score. The game starts when you go.

Tonight
  1. Pick a location with a clear eastern horizon. A hill, a park, an open field, a body of water. It does not need to be remote. It needs to be east-facing and accessible before sunrise.
  2. Look up tomorrow's sunrise time for that location. Set an alarm that gets you there at least 30 minutes before that time. 45 is better. An hour is better still.
  3. Charge whatever camera you have. A phone is sufficient. The Eos Index scores what the sky does; a phone captures that.
  4. Lay out what you are wearing and anything you are bringing. Nothing hunted for in the dark at 4AM.
  5. Set two alarms. One at wake time. One five minutes later.
Tomorrow Morning
  1. Get there early. Find your position. Get still. Let the landscape settle around you.
  2. Look around before you look east. Note what is at your feet, behind you, above you. The discovery window is open the moment you arrive.
  3. Let the sunrise happen. Do not spend it entirely behind your camera. Look at it. The score comes after.
  4. After sunrise, before you leave: note anything you saw or heard that could be a discovery. Take a photo of anything you cannot identify. Voice memo if your hands are occupied.
  5. Score it when you get home. Open the Eos Index rubric. Work through Sky, Setting, Conditions honestly. Add whatever travel and elevation points apply. Log any discoveries. Write down your total.

The first number you write down is the baseline. Every expedition after it is measured against that number, and against the growing record of what you have seen and how far you have gone. The leaderboard starts with one entry. Go make it.

A Note on Gear

You do not need to spend money to play this game. The Eos Index does not ask what camera you used. The Discovery Log does not ask how you got there. Elevation points are earned with legs. Pre-dawn arrival is earned with an alarm clock. Play the game you can play right now, with what you have. The leveling system will tell you when you are ready to go further.
