Foreword

Foreword

The Chronicle Begins

"Every dawn is a scored event. Most people sleep through the leaderboard."

This is not a travel guide. It is not a photography tutorial. It is not a nature journal. It is a game, played in the dark hours before the world decides what kind of day it wants to be.

The game has a name: Finding Zora. Zora is the Slavic word for dawn: that specific shade of possibility that exists only in the window between absolute darkness and the moment the sun commits to rising. Every expedition in this chronicle is an attempt to find it in its purest form.

The game is simple. Score the sunrise. Rate the effort it took to get there. Log what you discovered along the way. Add them up. That is your Zora Score. Do it again. Level up. The pursuit deepens the more you play.

This rulebook is the authoritative reference for how scores are calculated, how discoveries are logged, and how a player advances through the ranks. It is designed to be adopted by any individual player, group, or community who wants a structured, repeatable framework for the pursuit. One player or many. Any landscape. Any latitude. The rules apply wherever dawn breaks.

The game has no fixed home. It has been played in desert canyons and coastal cliffs, on mountain ridgelines and river banks, in landscapes the scorpions know and in latitudes they have never visited. Wherever the sun rises, the scoring system applies. The leaderboard is yours to build.

One final note before the rules begin. The scoring system rewards effort and intentionality, not luck. A spectacular sky witnessed from a hotel parking lot will always score lower than a modest sky earned at the top of a difficult pre-dawn ascent. The game knows the difference. So does the player.

Let the record show that every pursuit begins somewhere, before anyone is watching, in the dark.

Chapter I

The Three Pillars

"The Eos Index measures what the sky gave you. The Effort measures what you did to earn it. The Discovery Log measures what you found along the way."

Every expedition in Finding Zora produces a single number: the Zora Score. That number is built from three inputs, each measuring a different dimension of the chase.

100
Eos Index
The pure quality score of the sunrise itself. Sky, setting, and conditions combine to produce a number between 0 and 100. This is what the dawn gave you.
20
Effort Rating
A single post-expedition assessment of how hard you worked to be there. One slider, five levels. This is what the journey cost you.
variable
Discovery Points
Points earned from wildlife, flora, geographic features, and cultural sites encountered and documented during the expedition. This is what you found.
The Master Formula
Zora Score = Eos Index + Effort + Discovery Pts Three inputs. One score. A perfect Eos Index of 100 is the ultimate objective.

The Eos Index is always the foundation. The effort and discovery points reward the player for pushing further and seeing more, but the sunrise remains the heart of the game.

Core Principle

A perfect sunrise witnessed from a parking lot will always score lower than a modest sunrise earned at the top of a difficult pre-dawn ascent. The Eos Index measures the sky. The Effort rating and Discovery Log measure the player.

Chapter II

The Eos Index

"Eos, goddess of dawn, does not grade on a curve. Neither does this index."

The Eos Index is a scored assessment of sunrise quality, calculated across three components: Sky, Setting, and Conditions. The maximum possible score is 100 points. A score of 100 represents the theoretical perfect sunrise, the destination the game is named for.

Scores are assessed by the player in the field and may be finalized on review of any documentation gathered during the expedition: photographs, notes, and recorded observations. No score may be retroactively improved, but may be retroactively lowered if a review reveals an error in the original field assessment.

50
Sky
Color, cloud engagement, and horizon clarity. The dominant component.
30
Setting
Foreground composition and uniqueness of location.
20
Conditions
Effort to reach the position and environmental challenge.
Sky Component

I. Sky · 50 Points Maximum

The sky is the game. Everything else is context for the sky.

Sky Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts What earns a high score What earns a low score
Color Intensity 20 Deep reds, vivid oranges, saturated pinks across a wide chromatic range of the visible sky Pale yellow wash, minimal hue variation, muted or washed-out tones
Cloud Engagement 15 Dramatically lit clouds with visible rim-lighting, crepuscular rays, or layered cloud structures catching the first light Completely clear sky (moderate penalty) or flat uniform overcast with no visible detail (severe penalty)
Horizon Definition 15 Clean, readable horizon with clear separation between sky strata and earth: mountains, ridgelines, or open water at the edge Obstructed horizon (urban skyline, heavy haze), or no discernible horizon at all

Note on clear skies: a cloudless sunrise is not automatically a perfect sky. Clouds are the canvas upon which dawn paints her most memorable work. A perfectly clear sky earns a ceiling of approximately 35 out of 50 in the Sky component. The remaining 15 require clouds.

Setting Component

II. Setting · 30 Points Maximum

Where you are when the sun rises matters as much as what the sky does. An extraordinary sunrise witnessed from an extraordinary location is the mark of a well-planned expedition.

Setting Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts High score Low score
Foreground Composition 15 Compelling natural subject in frame: rock formation, ancient tree, waterfall, wildlife, or water reflection creating visual depth between viewer and horizon Featureless foreground, asphalt, suburban elements such as fences or buildings
Location Uniqueness 15 Remote wilderness, geological rarity, historically significant site, or a location requiring deliberate effort to access Suburban street, roadside pull-off, hotel parking lot, or any location accessible to the general public without planning
Conditions Component

III. Conditions · 20 Points Maximum

The Conditions component measures the effort to reach the chosen sunrise position and the environmental challenge encountered in doing so. This scores only the final approach and the conditions at the location itself.

Conditions Scoring Rubric
Sub-Component Max Pts High score Low score
Effort to Reach Position 10 Serious pre-dawn hike with elevation gain, technical terrain, or navigation challenge to reach the sunrise position Drive to location and stand beside the vehicle; roadside pull-off; flat paved walk under 0.25 miles
Environmental Challenge 10 Extreme cold, high wind, technical footing, rain, snow, or conditions that required genuine preparation and risk assessment Comfortable weather, no wind, clear footing: a perfect morning in every way except the challenge

Chapter III

The Effort Rating

"The sky rewards those who showed up. The Effort rating rewards those who had no business being there at all."

The Effort Rating is a single post-expedition assessment of the total difficulty of the journey. It replaces granular tracking of travel distance, elevation gain, weather conditions, and timing with one honest question: how hard did you work to be standing where you were when the sun rose?

The rating is set after the expedition is complete, when you are logging the outing. It is not calculated from data points. It is a judgment call, informed by what you experienced. The five levels are designed to be immediately recognizable. You will know which one fits.

Effort Rating Scale
LevelLabelPointsWhat this looks like
1 Roadside 0 pts Drove to a pulloff or parking lot and watched from beside the vehicle. Minimal planning, minimal physical effort. The sunrise came to you.
2 Trail 5 pts A deliberate outing with a real hike, a meaningful drive, or both. You chose a specific location and put in the work to reach it before dawn.
3 Summit 10 pts Significant physical effort: a serious pre-dawn ascent, a long approach over difficult terrain, or a combination of distance and elevation that required genuine commitment.
4 Remote 15 pts A location that required real logistics to reach: long travel, off-pavement driving, backcountry navigation, harsh weather, or conditions that made the expedition genuinely challenging beyond the hike itself.
5 Expedition 20 pts The full commitment: international travel, multi-day approaches, extreme terrain, severe weather, or a combination that represents the most the player has invested in reaching a single sunrise. Reserved for the outings that push the limits of the pursuit.
Guidance, Not Formulas

The Effort rating is intentionally subjective. A 40-mile drive over washboard dirt roads at 3AM might be a 3 for one player and a 4 for another, depending on their experience and the conditions that morning. There are no distance tables, elevation tiers, or timestamp verifications. Rate the effort honestly and move on. The game trusts the player.

The details that inform your effort rating — how far you drove, what elevation you gained, how early you arrived, what the weather did — are still worth noting in the expedition log. They tell the story of the outing and give the rating context. They just do not need to be individually scored. The story is the point. The number is the summary.

The Streak

The Consecutive Expedition Streak

The Streak is earned by completing all 6 outings for a level within 6 calendar weeks. It does not add points to the Zora Score. It is a visual honor only: a gold bar on the expedition share card, and a sunburst crown on the earned medallion — permanently distinguishing it from a non-streak medallion. The streak recognizes the discipline of sustained commitment rather than the outcome of any single outing.

Chapter IV

The Discovery Log

"You came for the sunrise. You stayed for the coyote. The ledger records both."

The Discovery Log is the living record of every species, geological formation, cultural site, and natural feature encountered across the pursuit. Every first encounter is an Unlock. Every Unlock adds points to the Zora Score of the expedition in which it occurs. Every Unlock is permanent and cannot be erased from the record.

The Discovery Log functions as a growing world map of encounters. Over the life of the pursuit, it becomes a documentary record of the natural and human history witnessed during the chase. This is where the game becomes something more than a game.

Discovery Categories

I. Four Discovery Categories

Discovery Category Reference
CategoryExamples
Wildlife Birds, mammals, reptiles, amphibians, insects: any living fauna encountered and documented
Flora Notable, rare, or species-specific plants: a desert wildflower bloom, an old-growth specimen, an endemic plant outside its known range
Geographic Geological formations, water bodies, natural arches, caves, lava fields, slot canyons: features of the earth itself
Cultural & Historical Petroglyphs, ruins, abandoned structures, ceremonial sites, mining history, indigenous land markers: evidence of human presence through time
Rarity Tiers

II. Rarity Tiers and Point Values

Every discovery is assigned a rarity tier at the time of logging. Tier assignment is based on regional rarity, difficulty of encounter, and documentary significance. Point values are fixed at the time of first unlock and cannot be changed retroactively.

Rarity Tier System
TierFirst UnlockSubsequent FindsWildlife Examples
Common 5 – 10 pts No repeat scoring Common Sparrow, Cottontail Rabbit, Mourning Dove
Uncommon 15 – 20 pts No repeat scoring Red-tailed Hawk, Great Blue Heron, White-tailed Deer, River Otter
Rare 25 – 35 pts +1 pt per outing Osprey, Peregrine Falcon, Bobcat, River Turtle
Very Rare 40 – 50 pts +1 pt per outing Bald Eagle, Gray Wolf, American Black Bear, Mountain Lion
Exceptional 60 – 75 pts +1 pt per outing Once-per-pursuit encounters, rare enough that no list can anticipate them
The Repeat Rule

Common and Uncommon discoveries score only on first encounter. Rare, Very Rare, and Exceptional discoveries earn one additional point for each subsequent encounter, provided the encounter occurs on a separate, distinct outing. Multiple encounters during the same expedition count as a single encounter.

Cultural & Historical Scoring

III. Cultural and Historical Sites

Cultural and historical discoveries are scored on significance rather than rarity tier. The following scale applies to first encounter only. Subsequent visits to the same site do not score additional points.

Cultural & Historical Discovery Scoring
Significance LevelPointsExamples
Minor 5 pts Historical marker, pioneer grave, named but unremarkable landmark
Notable 10 pts Intact ruins, mining camp, documented indigenous use site, named geographic landmark
Significant 15 pts Petroglyph panels, ceremonial sites, pre-Columbian structures, sites of documented historical events
Exceptional 20 pts UNESCO-adjacent significance, rare or previously undocumented sites, sites of major archaeological importance

IV. How an Unlock Is Logged

The following is an example of a properly logged discovery entry as it would appear in the Discovery Log:

Documentation Standard

A discovery is only eligible for points if it is documented with photographic or video evidence sufficient for confident identification. Blurry or inconclusive footage may be supported by audio recording (bird calls, etc.) in combination. The standard is: could a qualified naturalist confirm this identification from the footage provided?

Chapter V

The Zora Score

"Three numbers. One score. That is the whole game."

The Zora Score is the complete expedition score. It is the sum of the Eos Index, the Effort Rating, and any Discovery Points earned during the outing.

The Formula
Zora Score = Eos Index (0–100) + Effort (0–20) + Discoveries (variable) A perfect Eos Index of 100 = Finding Zora
Expected Ranges

To calibrate expectations, the following ranges reflect typical expedition outcomes:

Expected Zora Score Ranges
Expedition TypeExpected Zora Score
Local: nearby trail, average sunrise, few or no discoveries~50 – 70
Standard: deliberate outing, solid sunrise, several unlocks~70 – 90
Strong: remote destination, exceptional sunrise, multiple unlocks~90 – 120
Exceptional: maximum effort, rare discoveries, near-perfect sunrise~120 – 150+

Both the Eos Index and the Zora Score are recorded at the conclusion of every expedition. Both appear on the leaderboard. Players who care about sunrise photography follow the Eos Index. Players who care about the full adventure track the Zora Score. The game accommodates both.

The Expedition Log

What to record

When logging an expedition, record the following. The Eos Index and Effort rating produce the score. Everything else is context that enriches the record over time:

  1. Eos Index: Sky, Setting, Conditions breakdown and total
  2. Effort Rating: 1–5 level selection
  3. Discoveries: any unlocks, with rarity tier and point values
  4. Zora Score: the total
  5. Expedition notes: location, date, distance traveled, elevation, weather, arrival time, and anything else worth remembering. These details tell the story behind the number.
On Disputed Scores

The player is the sole authority on all scores. The scoring system is documented and transparent, but the player's field assessment is final. Arithmetic errors may be corrected at any time, with a note made in the record. No score may be retroactively improved; only corrected or lowered.

Chapter VI

The Leveling System

"Every six outings, if the player has shown up and done the work, something changes. A new medallion is earned. The pursuit deepens. The game expands."

The Leveling System is the long arc of the game. Where the Eos Index measures a single dawn and the Zora Score measures a single expedition, the Level measures the cumulative commitment of all expeditions: every predawn alarm, every cold trail, every sunrise logged in the record.

Leveling is driven purely by participation. Each level requires 6 completed expeditions. Score has no effect on progression — show up, do the work, level up. The Zora Score drives leaderboard position; expedition count drives leveling. Every level earned is a milestone, and what that milestone means in practice is entirely up to the player.

The Math

I. The Math

Each level requires exactly 6 expeditions. There are 10 earned levels, for a total of 60 expeditions from Scout to Finding Zora. No shortcuts. No score thresholds. Just consistent pursuit.

Level-Up Ceremony

III. The Level-Up Moment

When the 6th expedition for a level is completed, the player has earned the next medallion. The level-up moment includes the following:

  1. Expedition count noted (e.g., "Expedition 12 — Level 2 earned")
  2. New level title claimed
  3. Medallion earned and added to the display
  4. Personal milestone acknowledged: the player decides what this level means to them and what, if anything, to do differently as a result

Players who document their pursuit publicly may choose to announce level-ups as part of their record. The current level and progress toward the next medallion should be visible in whatever tracking system the player maintains.

Chapter VII

The Records Board

"Records exist to be broken. That is the only reason the board is kept."

The Records Board tracks the all-time bests across a defined set of categories. Unlike the leaderboards (which rank expeditions by Eos Index or Zora Score), Records track specific achievements that stand on their own.

A record, once set, belongs to the expedition that set it. When a record is broken, the previous holder is noted. Records tell the story of the pursuit's ambition over time.

Chapter VIII

Field Protocol

"The protocol exists because the wilderness does not wait for the unready. Set your clock the night before."

The Field Protocol is a framework for running an expedition. It is not a rigid script — the wilderness does not honor scripts — but a structure that ensures consistent documentation and the best possible chance of witnessing something worth recording.

Pre-Expedition

I. The Night Before

  1. Confirm sunrise time for your target location and date
  2. Check weather forecast: note cloud cover probability (30–60% is the sweet spot for the Eos Index; clear is moderate; full overcast is poor)
  3. Research recent wildlife sightings for the target location if resources are available. Know what's possible before arrival
  4. Charge all equipment the night before: cameras, lights, and any recording devices
  5. Lay out gear and clothing. Nothing gets searched for in the dark at 4AM
  6. Set two alarms: one at wake time, one five minutes later
In the Field

II. The Discovery Window

The period between arrival and sunrise is the primary discovery window. Wildlife and features encountered here become the expedition's discovery points.

  1. Move slowly and deliberately; high-movement approaches clear wildlife from the area
  2. Photograph or document every potential discovery before attempting identification
  3. Log wildlife and plant sightings in real time. Do not rely on memory
  4. Reach your sunrise position with time to spare. Stillness produces more encounters than movement
The Sunrise

III. Sunrise Protocol

  1. Lock down your primary camera on a stable mount, facing east, before sunrise begins
  2. Begin your continuous sunrise recording. Do not interrupt this for any reason short of equipment failure. This is the Eos Index source material.
  3. Use any secondary cameras for supplementary coverage while the primary runs
  4. Note your real-time field assessment of sky conditions as a reference for the formal scoring
The Verdict

IV. The Verdict

The formal closing assessment of every expedition:

  1. Eos Index breakdown: Sky, Setting, Conditions scores with brief justification
  2. Effort Rating: select the level that fits the expedition
  3. Discovery summary: all unlocks from this expedition, point values, running total
  4. Zora Score total: this expedition's final score
  5. Expedition count: running total, progress toward next medallion
  6. Level-up acknowledgement: if applicable
  7. Records broken: if applicable

Appendix B

Glossary of Terms

"Know the language of the pursuit."

Blue Hour
The 20–30 minute window before sunrise when the sky transitions from deep navy to pale gold. Often more visually striking than sunrise itself. The primary pre-positioning window for the player to reach and settle into their sunrise location.
Calibration Session
An optional pre-season scoring exercise in which existing photographs or documentation are scored using the Eos Index rubric to establish personal leaderboard benchmarks and test scoring consistency before formal play begins.
Cumulative Zora Score
The running total of all Zora Scores across all expeditions. Drives leaderboard position. Level progression is determined by expedition count, not score.
Discovery Window
The period between arrival at a location and official sunrise time. The primary window for wildlife encounters and feature discovery.
Effort Rating
A post-expedition assessment of the total difficulty of the journey, rated on a 1–5 scale (Roadside, Trail, Summit, Remote, Expedition). Contributes 0–20 points to the Zora Score.
Eos Index
The pure sunrise quality score for a single episode. Scored across three components (Sky, Setting, Conditions) with a maximum of 100 points. Named for Eos, the Greek goddess of dawn.
Exceptional Tier
The highest rarity tier in the Discovery Log. Encounters so rare that no definitive list can anticipate them. Worth 60–75 points on first unlock.
Finding Zora
The name of this game system and the pursuit it governs. Also the name of Level 10, the theoretical culmination of the pursuit. Also the ultimate objective of the Eos Index: a perfect score of 100. A perfect sunrise.
First Unlock
The first confirmed encounter with a species, feature, or cultural site that has not previously appeared in the Discovery Log. Earns the full point value for that rarity tier.
Streak
Earned by completing all 6 outings for a level within 6 calendar weeks. A visual honor only — displayed as a gold bar on the expedition share card and a sunburst crown on the earned medallion. Does not contribute points to the Zora Score.
The Level-Up Moment
The formal acknowledgement when a level's 6th expedition is completed. Includes recording the new level title, noting the expedition count, earning the medallion, and reflecting on the personal milestone the player has chosen to associate with this level.
The Records Board
The all-time best achievements tracking board, separate from the Eos Index and Zora Score leaderboards. Tracks notable achievements like longest streak and most unusual location.
The Verdict
The structured closing assessment of every expedition where all scores are announced, discoveries logged, and progress updated.
Unlock
A first confirmed encounter with a species, geographic feature, or cultural site. Once unlocked, an entry appears permanently in the Discovery Log.
Zora
The Slavic word for dawn. The name of the pursuit, the game, and the theoretical destination at the end of the leaderboard. A perfect sunrise. The one that scores 100.
Zora Score
The complete expedition score for a single outing. The sum of the Eos Index, the Effort Rating, and any Discovery Points earned during the expedition.

Appendix C

House Rules

"The rules exist. They also bend. Know the difference."

The standard rulebook is designed to work as written, for any player, in any landscape, without modification. But the game acknowledges that players arrive with different histories and may want to adjust the structure to fit their pursuit. The following house rules are officially recognized variants. Any house rule in use should be declared at the start of a season and noted in the player's record.

Calibration as Season Zero
House Rule · Calibration as Season Zero

A player with an existing library of documented sunrise expeditions may run a Season Zero before formal play begins. In Season Zero, the player scores up to ten prior expeditions under the full rulebook. Each scored expedition counts toward the player's expedition total, determining their starting level (6 expeditions per level). The resulting Zora Scores establish the baseline leaderboard for Season One. Season Zero expeditions are marked as calibration in the log.

Group Play
House Rule · Group Play

When two or more players undertake the same expedition, each player maintains their own Eos Index assessment, Effort Rating, and Zora Score. The Discovery Log may be maintained as either individual (each player logs their own first-unlock entries independently) or shared (the group maintains a single communal log, and first-unlock points are awarded to the player who first documented the encounter). Declare the variant before the expedition begins.

Photography-Only Mode
House Rule · Photography-Only Mode

Players focused exclusively on sunrise quality may elect to play in Photography-Only Mode. In this variant, only the Eos Index is scored. Effort and discovery points are not tracked. The leaderboard consists solely of Eos Index scores. Well suited to stationary observers, photographers working a single location over time, or groups that want a common scoring language without the logistics of full expedition play.

Discovery Disputes
House Rule · Discovery Tier Disputes

When a player is uncertain of the appropriate rarity tier for a discovery, the default is to assign conservatively (the lower of the two candidate tiers) and note the dispute in the log. The assignment may be revised upward at any time with additional documentation or corroborating expert confirmation. In group play, tier disputes are resolved by majority vote; ties default to the lower tier.

Appendix D

Your First Expedition

"You do not need perfect conditions. You need to be somewhere with a clear view east before the sun rises. Everything else is optional the first time."

You have read the rulebook. The scoring system may feel large. It is not complicated in practice. Your first expedition does not need to produce a great score. It needs to produce a score. The game starts when you go.

Tonight
  1. Pick a location with a clear eastern horizon. A hill, a park, an open field, a body of water. It does not need to be remote. It needs to be east-facing and accessible before sunrise.
  2. Look up tomorrow's sunrise time for that location. Set an alarm that gets you there at least 30 minutes before that time. 45 is better. An hour is better still.
  3. Charge whatever camera you have. A phone is sufficient. The Eos Index scores what the sky does; a phone captures that.
  4. Lay out what you are wearing and anything you are bringing. Nothing hunted for in the dark at 4AM.
  5. Set two alarms. One at wake time. One five minutes later.
Tomorrow Morning
  1. Get there early. Find your position. Get still. Let the landscape settle around you.
  2. Look around before you look east. Note what is at your feet, behind you, above you. The discovery window is open the moment you arrive.
  3. Let the sunrise happen. Do not spend it entirely behind your camera. Look at it. The score comes after.
  4. After sunrise, before you leave: note anything you saw or heard that could be a discovery. Take a photo of anything you cannot identify.
When You Get Home
  1. Score the Eos Index. Work through Sky, Setting, Conditions honestly.
  2. Set your Effort Rating. How hard was it to be there? Pick the level that fits.
  3. Log any discoveries. Assign rarity tiers, note the points.
  4. Add it up. That is your Zora Score. Write it down.

The first number you write down is the baseline. Every expedition after it is measured against that number, and against the growing record of what you have seen and how far you have gone. The leaderboard starts with one entry. Go make it.

A Note on Gear

You do not need to spend money to play this game. The Eos Index does not ask what camera you used. The Discovery Log does not ask how you got there. The Effort rating is earned with legs and an alarm clock. Play the game you can play right now, with what you have. The leveling system will tell you when you are ready to go further.
